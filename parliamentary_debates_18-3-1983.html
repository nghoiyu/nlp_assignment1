CRIME INVESTIGATION AND CONTROL
(Clearance rate for criminal offences)


    2. Mr Goh Chee Wee asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) what was the clearance rate of all the criminal offences committed in Singapore in 1980, 1981, and 1982; and (b) what measures his Ministry will take to improve the effectiveness of crime investigation and control.

     The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Chua Sian Chin): Mr Speaker, Sir, the overall clearance rate for criminal cases in Singapore is as follows: 1980 28%; 1981 33%; 1982 29%.

    Our overall clearance rate for criminal cases is comparable to that of other highly developed countries in the world. For example, the overall clearance rate in Australia is 28.5%, Denmark is 23.2%, and the United States of America is 19.5%. However, among the developed countries Japan has achieved the highest overall clearance rate of 63.8%. This has been attributed to their koban system of policing. The koban police is responsible for more than 60% of the total number of criminal cases cleared in Japan. The Japanese koban police has been able to do this because of the excellent relationship they have with the residents in the neighbourhood, which is based on complete confidence and trust. As a result, the Japanese police has no difficulty in getting information on the presence of criminals, of crimes committed and getting the residents to be witnesses. Therefore, to improve the effectiveness of crime investigation and control, and thus the clearance rate of cases, it is not only necessary to upgrade the professional skills of police officers in crime investigation but, what is even more important, to forge a relationship of mutual trust and confidence between the police and the public so that members of the public will not hesitate to help the police in their investigations.

Column: 1134



NPP System

    The most important measure that my Ministry will take to achieve this objective is the introduction of the Neighbourhood Police Post (NPP) System. The NPP System is modelled after the Japanese koban system. It will ensure that police officers will always be close to all the residents and continually interact with them. An important feature of the NPP System is the home visit by NPP officers every six months to get to know the residents, listen to their views and suggestions and to offer their services. With the establishment of trust in the police officers, we can expect the vast majority of the residents to come forward to freely confide in the police officers and to volunteer information on the presence of criminals and strangers behaving suspiciously in the neighbourhood.

Crime Prevention

    The Police, however, is not relying solely on the NPP System to achieve the aim of total involvement with the community and its grassroots organizations. In the last two years, the first steps towards this objective have been taken with the introduction of the Neighbourhood Watch Scheme (NWS), the establishment of the National Crime Prevention Council and the formation of Boys' Clubs. The Crime Prevention Department has also made surveys of commercial premises with a view to advising the management and occupiers to get together to form crime prevention committees and implement crime prevention measures.

Upgrading of Skills

    The Police is also upgrading the professional skills of police officers in criminal investigation and other crime control activities. A team of British experts was asked to look into ways and means of improving the training facilities and the standard of instruction at the Police Academy. Many of their recommendations are now in the process of being implemented. Currently, the Police Academy runs many in-house courses on criminal investigation. Our crime investigators are also sent overseas for specialized training and attachments to learn new skills, particularly those relating to the investigation of commercial crime.

Computerization

    To enhance the effectiveness of the Force in crime control, the Police is also implementing large scale computerization and mechanization of police operations. Command and control of patrol resources will be computerized by 1985. This computerized system will optimize the deployment of patrols and the allocation of resources in response to calls for Police assistance. Case management will be computerized by 1986 to ensure quicker processing and closer monitoring of crime cases.

    Besides these, the Police is also exploring other ways and means of mechanization and computerization such as in the lifting and matching of fingerprints and the collation of criminal intelligence. These will further help to improve the effectiveness of criminal investigations.

    3.     The Minister of State for Law and Home Affairs (Prof. S. Jayakumar) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Sir, expatriates intending to take up employment in Singapore have to apply to the Immigration Department for Employment Passes if their monthly salaries exceed $1,000 per month. Applications are made in the prescribed forms and submitted through the employing company or a local sponsor. The applicant has to describe in the application form, his qualifications, working experience, duties to be performed and the details of the company which intends

to engage his services. These forms are designed to enable the Immigration Department to obtain full information on the applicant's background and experience. The advice of relevant vetting authorities is also obtained before a decision is made. The main consideration in approving Employment Passes for expatriates is whether they are of economic benefit to Singapore.

    The applicant's qualifications and experience are the main factors which the Immigration Department considers in assessing each application for Employment Pass. The Department works within policy guidelines which have been drawn up by the Committee on Attracting Talents to Singapore (CATS). The Committee includes representatives from the Ministry of Trade and Industry and EDB. Amongst other things, the Committee is responsible for the identification, attraction and assimilation of non-Singaporean talent.

    When there is doubt about the professional standing or technical competence of the applicant, the Department will refer the application to the relevant Ministry or Department for advice. For example, if the applicant is from the banking sector, his application would be referred to the Monetary Authority of Singapore for vetting.

    Statistical data on the stock of Employment Pass holders, and on Employment Pass applications received, approved and rejected, are sent regularly to the Ministry of Trade and Industry for monitoring purposes.

    Each year Immigration Department receives thousands of applications for Employment Pass. The average number of applications per year for the last three years (1980-1982) was 21,820. The Immigration Department can only assess the applications based on information given by the applicants in the application forms and certified true by the employers or the sponsors. In most cases, the applications are decided within a month. In processing the applications, the officers will scrutinize the details in the application forms, and ask for documentary evidence to substantiate the statements made in the forms.

During these meetings, the details on the restructure of the GES and the amendments of its rules, by-laws and regulations to tighten control over its members were discussed.

    The GES in its Extraordinary General Meeting on 11 February 1983 has approved the proposed amendments to its Articles of Association drawn up by its Management.

    The main changes to the GES Articles of Association and rules are:-

    a. Legal status of the GES

    The GES, formerly a private company incorporated under the Companies Act, has been converted into a public limited company to cater for an enlarged membership.(Under the Companies Act, a private company can only have a maximum of 50 shareholders/members.)

    b. GES Committee

    The GES Committee is the main executive body of the exchange and has been vested with all the powers of the exchange for the management of the affairs of the exchange. Membership of this

    Committee has been increased from six to eight members of which two will be representatives from the MAS. The remaining six will be elected by members in a General Meeting from the candidates nominated. The nominees will have to be approved by the MAS. The objective of this

    amendment is to enable the MAS to play a more direct role in the initial supervision and development of the GES.

    c. Sub-Committee

    Two sub-committees, each with three members have been formed. These are the Membership Sub-Committee and Disciplinary Sub-Committee, which will take charge of membership applications and disciplinary matters respectively.

    d. Disciplinary Action and Appeal

    The principle of 'show cause' has been introduced as a new rule for disciplinary action. If any member (clearing and non-clearing) fails to comply with any of the GES Articles, rules, by-laws or regulations, the Disciplinary Sub-Committee would require the member concerned to 'show cause' as to why he should not be reprimanded, suspended or expelled or be subject to such other action as the Sub-Committee may deem fit. A member who considers himself aggrieved by any decision of the Disciplinary Sub-Committee may appeal to the GES Committee and the decision of the Committee shall be final.

    e. Discretionary Accounts

    A new provision has been incorporated in this rule to discourage the operation of discretionary accounts except when a member has obtained specific approval from the GES Committee. Approval for the operation of discretionary accounts will only be given under strict terms and conditions. The MAS has asked the GES to propose a standard format of agreement for the operation of discretionary accounts for its approval.

    f. Auditing Requirements

    A new provision has also been incorporated in the GES rules giving the GES Committee power to appoint auditors at any time to inspect the books of its members.

    g. Changes in the Ownership and Control of Member Company

    The amended Article of the GES would require a member to seek approval from the Membership Sub-Committee before any change in the legal or beneficial ownership or change in the control of the member company can take place. This is to ensure that an unscrupulous operator would not become a member by buying into an existing member company.

    The amendments will empower the GES to supervise and regulate its members and to cater for the expansion and development of the exchange. A technical sub-committee was appointed to make further study of the by-laws and regulations which will govern the business conduct and practices of gold trading.

    Some of the proposals approved by the MAS are:-

    a. To extend the by-laws and regulations to cover the conduct of associate members of the GES. Previously, they applied only to full members of the GES.

    b. To introduce a by-law to prohibit the practice of 'churning' of the accounts of customers. 'Churning' is the buying and selling of contracts by the broker at the same price. Some brokers have been engaging in the practice of 'churning' of the accounts of their customers in order to earn more commission.

    c. a new by-law would be added to ensure that all kerb trading are reported promptly. Kerb trades are deals done after GES trading hours (that is, transactions done at night) and which are to be reported the following day. Certain guidelines would be drawn up by the exchange to monitor kerb trading and to ensure that kerb trades are actually done at the proper market price range.

    d. a new provision would be included to disallow firms from misrepresenting facts to the clients.

    The technical sub-committee is also studying the feasibility of imposing segregated account requirements for the protection of clients. This is to ensure that clients' funds, placed with the gold trading company as margin deposit, are kept separately from the members' so that the investors' interest would be protected.

    The GES is currently studying a proposal to expand into financial futures trading activities. In this connection, the possibility of an institutional link with an overseas exchange for trading in both gold and financial futures is under study. A project manager from the Chicago Mercantile Exchange is now in Singapore studying the details of the link- up between Singapore and the International Monetary Market. There are advantages in establishing such a link but the problems are not inconsiderable.

    I would like to point out that the changes that have been made only cover the main areas of concern. As the exchange matures and gains more experience, more rules, by-laws and regulations would be amended or introduced to ensure the development of an orderly and efficient market.

    May I end on a word of caution. The gold market and more especially the futures market, here as elsewhere, will attract speculators. Speculators are usually people with strong gambling propensities. While regulations properly enforced can curb the worst excesses of speculation, there is no way the Government can save people who are determined to part with their money from the consequences of their own folly. The reports on the 24 gold trading companies, should my colleague the Minister for Law decide to publish them, will bear me out on this point.
