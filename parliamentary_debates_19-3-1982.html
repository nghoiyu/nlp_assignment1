EMPLOYMENT ACT
(Protection against dismissals)


    2. Mr Liew Kok Pun asked the Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour (a) whether he is satisfied that the present Employment Act gives sufficient protection to a worker with respect to his tenure of appointment; (b) whether he is aware that there are certain sections of the Act that leave workers completely at the mercy of the employer; and (c) whether he thinks it is necessary to have a law against unfair dismissal.

     The Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour (Mr Ong Teng Cheong): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Employment Act provides adequate protection for employees against unfair termination or dismissal. Employees who feel that they have been terminated or dismissed without just cause or excuse can appeal to the Minister for redress. If the Minister is satisfied that the dismissal has been made without just cause or excuse, the employer will be ordered either to reinstate the employee or to pay compensation. The maximum penalty on the employer for not heeding the Minister's order for reinstatement or compensation is a $5,000 fine or imprisonment up to 12 months, or both.

    The rights and obligations of employees and employers are clearly spelt out in the Act. Since its promulgation, the Act has been operating smoothly in safeguarding the interest and welfare of employees.

    Over the past five years a total of 141 appeals against dismissal were received. In 87 of these cases, our investigations

Column: 1235

confirmed that the dismissals were justified.

    In 10 cases, however, the Ministry ruled that the dismissals were wrongful.In some of these instances, the dismissals were carried out without a proper inquiry or without grounds to substantiate the charge. Reinstatement of the dismissed workers was ordered by the Minister in five cases and compensation was paid out in the other five cases. The remaining 44 cases were amicably settled through conciliation at the Ministry. In the majority of these cases, during conciliation the dismissed workers accepted the dismissal and the employers also agreed to grant compensation as both parties were found to be partially wrong in their actions. From these figures, it is clear that employers generally have not abused their rights in terminating or dismissing employees. It is, therefore, unnecessary to modify the current law governing dismissal.

INDUSTRIAL ACCIDENTS


    3. Mr Liew Kok Pun asked the Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour (a) how many accidents have occurred in the various industries in Singapore over the past five years; (b) what measures the Ministry of Labour has taken to reduce these accidents; and (c) whether he is satisfied that the Ministry is doing enough to ensure that the lives of workers are not unnecessarily put in danger.

     Dr Wong Kwei Cheong (for the Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour): Mr Speaker, Sir, there were a total of 4,575 accidents reported to the Factory Inspectorate in 1977 as compared with 4,554 in 1978, 5,277 in 1979, 6,087 in 1980 and 6,046 in 1981. Although the number of accidents has increased over the years, the industrial safety situation has not deteriorated. The increasing number of accidents has to be interpreted in the light of the increasing number of workers employed in industry and the resultant increased exposure to risk in terms of man-hours worked. To give proper weight to these factors, the accident severity and frequency rates have to be calculated to establish if the industrial accident situation

Column: 1238

has improved or deteriorated. The accident severity rates in 1977 was 501 mandays lost per million man-hours worked compared with 504 man-days lost per million man-hours worked in 1981. The accident frequency rates for the five years from 1977 to 1981 were kept within the limits of 5.0 and 6.5 accidents per million man-hours worked. Hence, in these terms, it can be said that the industrial accident situation has not deteriorated.

    My Ministry has taken various measures to improve the industrial accident situation. These measures are as follows:-

     (a) Safety standards

    One measure is to set safety standards to be observed in the industry. These have been prescribed through legislation. Special safety requirements are also laid down to protect workers who are engaged in work which may expose them to risks. For instance, there are special safety provisions for workers who are exposed to the risks of falling objects or debris from heights, asphyxiation and fire and explosion. These standards and requirements are being reviewed continually to ensure that they are up-to-date and relevant.

     (b) Safety Officers

    Certain classes of factories have also been required to employ safety officers and to establish safety committees. These safety officers help to identify hazardous situations and make recommendations to management to rectify them. They also help to implement safety measures.

     (c) Enforcement

    To ensure that safety standards for the protection of workers are complied with, continuous enforcement action has to be taken. To increase efficiency, selective enforcement in factories and industries which are more hazardous has been practised.

     (d) Advisory Committees

    In addition, the Ministry has set up Advisory Committees to look into the special problems encountered by the ship-building and ship-repairing industry and the construction industry, so that appropriate recommendations on preventive measures can be made.

    Furthermore, numerous safety training courses have been conducted for safety personnel, workers, supervisors and management. Special mass orientation and safety instruction courses have also been conducted for workers in the shipbuilding and ship-repairing and the construction industries.
