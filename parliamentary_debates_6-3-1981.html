SUBSIDY FOR ELECTRICITY
AND WATER CHARGES


    14. Encik Mansor Haji Sukaimi asked the Minister for Trade and Industry and Minister for Health what is the exact quantum of subsidy paid by Government for a unit block of electricity and water consumed by industries and the household and how does this element of subsidy in Singapore compare with subsidies in other countries.

     Mr Goh Chok Tong: Mr Speaker, Sir, the Singapore Government does not subsidize electricity and water charges. Let me explain why we adopt such a policy.

    We import all our oil to generate electricity. The oil producers do not subsidize us. We have to pay the full cost of oil we import. If we subsidize consumers of electricity, we may also have to subsidize bus fares and other services which depend on oil. Where do we find money to pay for these subsidies? The Singapore Government does not print money. If we need more money, we have to raise it through taxes, which means that

Column: 309

ultimately it is the people themselves who have to pay for subsidies, not the Government.

    Another reason: if consumers do not pay the full price of utilities, they will inevitably over-consume. In other words, it is possible for them to live beyond their incomes in actual terms, since part of their consumption is subsidized.

    If we all live beyond our actual capacity to earn incomes, we shall soon find ourselves unable to pay for the full market price of oil. If utilities are not subsidized, we do not have to fear that we are unable to pay for the cost of importing oil because for every barrel of oil we import, the full cost is recovered from the consumers.

    The same arguments against subsidy apply to water.

Singapore's Economy in 1980

    Singapore's economy grew robustly in 1980, achieving a double-digit rate of growth of over 10% since the first oil crisis of 1973-74. Singaporeans have from all walks of life once again shown their remarkable capacity to overcome great odds. We are restructuring our economy in an inhospitable international economic climate. We did better than 1979 despite slow growth and high inflation in a world economy beset by the sudden contraction in oil supplies following the Iranian Revolution and the Iranian-iraqi War.

    Business enterprises trimmed and streamlined their organizations, shedding off excess baggage. They mechanized and automated. The Civil Service and statutory boards also rationalized. The Government pushed with higher wages, and pulled with fiscal incentives. Labour productivity surged forward, doubling to 5%. Had there been no productivity gains, the economy would have grown by only 5%, instead of over 10%.

(i) Separate Assessment on Unearned Incomes of Married Women

    Option for separate assessment on earned incomes was first given to married women in 1963. It is clear today that many married women are capable of deriving unearned incomes in their own right. (ii) Relief for Disabled Persons

    This is the International Year of Disabled Persons. As our contribution, I am increasing the earned income relief for handicapped persons from $1,000 to $2,000. (iv) Estate Duty
