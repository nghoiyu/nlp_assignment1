ORAL ANSWER TO QUESTION
IMPORTED MOTOR VEHICLES
(Safety and pollution control standards)


    1. Mr Tang See Chim asked the Acting Minister for Communications whether he is satisfied that motor vehicles imported into Singapore meet with the required standards of safety and pollution control, and what are those standards.

     The Acting Minister for Communications (Dr Yeo Ning Hong): Mr Speaker, Sir, the prototype of every new model of motor vehicle imported into Singapore is subjected to an inspection by the Registry of Vehicles to ensure that the vehicle meets the pollution and safety standards as specified in the Motor Vehicles (Construction and Use) Rules, 1974.

    In addition, imported second-hand vehicles must be inspected individually before they are allowed to be registered.

     Mr Tang See Chim (Chua Chu Kang): Mr Speaker, Sir, what about the question of pollution control?

     Dr Yeo Ning Hong: Sir, as I have stated in my answer, the pollution standards meet the specifications.

     Mr Tang See Chim: What are those specifications?

     Dr Yeo Ning Hong: Sir, I have all the details of the specifications. I would be happy to provide them to the Member for Chua Chu Kang if he should so require.

     Mr Tang See Chim: Sir, is the Minister aware that in two separate accidents recently, two cars split into two? Does this mean that those makes of cars meet with the required safety standards?

     Dr Yeo Ning Hong: Sir, those two accidents had nothing to do with pollution. Immediately after the accidents, ROV and the Traffic Police carried out detailed investigations. The investigations suggest that the accidents occurred at extremely high speeds at the point of impact. ROV had caused technical teams from the manufacturers to come to Singapore to study the accidents and to report on them.

INSTITUTE OF EDUCATION (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister of State of Education (Dr Tay Eng Soon): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The amendment to the Institute of Education Act is connected mainly with my Ministry's plan to set up a Physical Education College. In 1969, Professor Sasaki, a Japanese expert in Physical Education, visited Singapore to study our school physical education programme. Among some of his observations of our school children, he noted that they were not getting enough strenuous exercise. Many of our school fields were empty compared with Japanese school fields. He also commented:

    "Compared to Japanese children, there are many slim boys and girls in Singapore."

He was referring to their thin arms and legs.

    That was in 1969, 15 years ago. Today, with better diet, our children are not as slim as when Professor Sasaki saw them. But the need for a proper physical education programme in our schools continues to exist. The need is not only to improve

Column: 410

the physical fitness of our urban flat-dwelling children and to prepare the boys to be more fit for National Service. Good physical education would improve overall the health of our people and cut down on our national health cost. The recreational and social aspects of physical education would have far-reaching benefits as our society becomes more affluent and can afford leisure activities.

    Professor Sasaki recommended that a physical education college should be set up to train full-time physical education teachers if we wanted to give physical education its due priority in our schools. Unfortunately, we had many other more pressing matters in education in the years after the Sasaki report. However, three years ago, we decided to address the problem of physical education and to give it due priority in our schools. Our major constraint is not enough qualified physical education teachers. At present, we have only 60 physical education teachers who have undergone full-time courses overseas. A well-run physical education programme in our secondary schools and junior colleges alone would require 700 full-time physical education teachers.

    To meet this need, my Ministry has decided to set up a College of Physical Education. An autonomous college would be able to focus its full attention on all aspects of physical education. It will not only be training teachers for our schools. It will also be working with bodies such as the Singapore Armed Forces, the Singapore Sports Council, the People's Association and the Police to meet their physical education manpower needs. As leisure needs in Singapore increase in the future, it is conceivable that coaches, sports club administrators and others will be in demand. The Physical Education College would have a growing role as a centre for training and research.

    The planning for the new Physical Education College is in an advanced stage and key personnel have been appointed. The first intake of about 50 students will take place in July this year. The College will be located at the Institute of Education campus at Bukit Timah Road with which it will be sharing facilities.

    On 23rd September, 1983, a motortrailer carrying piling machinery hit the bottom part of a pedestrian overhead bridge along Upper Thomson Road.
