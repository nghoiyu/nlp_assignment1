EDUCATION OF HANDICAPPED CHILDREN


    2. Dr Tan Cheng Bock asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education why his Ministry is not willing to shoulder the responsibility of educating our handicapped children like the deaf, the blind and the retarded and whether such a move will deprive them of the best education possible.

     The Minister of State of Education (Dr Tay Eng Soon) (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am replying on behalf of my Minister.

    Far from being unwilling to shoulder the responsibility, the Government has and will continue to give substantial assistance to voluntary organizations in the education of handicapped children. The Government's policy is to encourage the efforts of the voluntary organizations which are running the special schools and to support them wherever possible. The Ministry of Education has been providing teachers to teach in the eight special schools. Similarly, the Ministry of Social Affairs has been providing instructors for the School for the Blind and the School for the Deaf. In addition, the Ministries of Social Affairs and Health give annual grants to the special schools to meet part of their recurrent expenditure.

    In 1982, 69 government teachers and 11 instructors were sent to the special schools. The Ministry of Education had also agreed to deploy another 25 teachers to the special schools last year. The total government subsidy per pupil in the special schools taking into account the grants provided, the teachers and instructors provided was $1,626 per pupil in 1982. This is 41% more than the subsidy of $1,153 per pupil in our regular primary schools in 1982.

The Ministry is also assisting the Singapore Association for Retarded Children to take over the Griffiths School building for the Association's use on a nominal rental basis.

    Some handicapped children with normal intelligence attend regular schools at both primary and secondary levels. There are nine visually-handicapped pupils and 59 pupils with hearing impairment studying in our regular primary schools. At the secondary level, 36 visually-handicapped students are studying in seven regular secondary schools and 63 students with hearing impairment in two regular secondary schools. Six resource teachers are provided to assist these students. The students attend regular classes with normal students and follow the normal curriculum. Taxi drivers are obliged to stop when flagged unless they have passengers in their taxis or a 'NOT FOR HIRE' sign has been displayed. However, sometimes taxi drivers did not stop because they did not notice that there were commuters by the roadside flagging for their taxis as they need also to pay attention to traffic conditions on the road. Therefore, complaints of this nature need to be carefully investigated by ROV.

The Economy in 1982

    A detailed review of the performance of our economy in 1982 can be found in the Economic Survey of Singapore, 1982 (Cols. 447 - 728), copies of which have been given to hon. Members of the House earlier. Today I shall highlight our performance briefly.

    In my Budget Statement last year, I told the House that I expected 1982 to be a grim year with mounting problems of unemployment and protectionism in the industrialized countries. To my regret, my fears have turned out to be well-founded. The world economy is today in a most perilous state. The hopes of many finance ministers, bankers and economists were dashed when the US economy did not recover and consequently failed to pull the world out of the recession. Instead, the US economy contracted by 1.8%. Growth in the other industrialized countries, where it did occur, was muted. In November last year Trade Ministers from 88 countries attended a meeting of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT) in an attempt to halt and to roll back protectionism. NATION-WIDE POWER FAILURE ON 5TH FEBRUARY, 1983


    17. Mr S. Chandra Das asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) what cause or causes led to the major nation-wide power failure on Saturday, 5th February, 1983; and (b) what steps will be taken to prevent a similar occurrence in the future.

     Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam: I would like to take advantage of this question to explain not only what caused the island-wide power failure on Saturday, 5th February, 1983, but also why it took many hours to restore full supply, and finally what we can do to minimize disruption and danger to life in the event of a power failure.

    The power failure on 5th February, 1983, occurred as a result of a severe short circuit at a new section of the 230 kilovolt switchboard being installed and tested at Senoko Power Station Stage III which is under construction.

    The short circuit was so severe that it created a surge of power equivalent to five times the power being generated to supply the entire Republic at that time. The extreme overload severely disturbed the electricity supply system and led to the tripping (automatic disconnection from the system) in rapid succession of the generators at Jurong, Senoko and Pasir Panjang Power Stations. All the power stations were affected because they are interlinked through the Republic's electricity supply network.

Column: 437



    The power failure did not occur because of insufficient generating capacity. At present, the daily peak demand for electricity is about 1,350 MW but the total generating capacity of all the power stations is about 2,100 MW. There is therefore more than sufficient generating capacity to cater for Singapore's demand.

    Let me now explain our electricity supply system and why it took several hours to restore full supply to the island. In a power station fuel is burned in the boilers which produce high pressure steam. This steam is used to drive the turbine-generators to generate electricity. Electricity generated at power stations is fed to four major 230 kilovolt substations through the 230 kilovolt cable transmission system. From there, electricity is distributed to consumers via some 3,600 substations and more than 33,000 km of their associated cable systems. This system of inter-linking power stations and substations through cables constitutes our electricity supply network.

    Like all major electricity supply systems, Singapore's power stations are linked through the network. This has been found by practice to be the most efficient and economical mode of operation and affords flexibility and reliability. During a planned shutdown for overhaul and maintenance of equipment at one power station, electricity can be supplied from the other power stations. More important, if there is a fault in one power station causing a generator to trip, the other power stations with reserve capacity (or, in technical jargon, spinning reserve) will automatically take over the load thereby preventing power failure.

    There is, however, an attendant disadvantage in such a system in that an extraordinary fault at one power station may affect the other power stations by virtue of the fact that they are linked. On 5th February, 1983, such an extraordinary fault occurred, causing such a severe overload that it tripped out all the generators in operation within seconds, nullifying the back-up system of spinning reserve.

    When there is a total collapse of the electricity supply system, restoration of supply takes time because established safety procedures have to be followed. When the generators trip, the boilers go out and the fuel pipes to the boiler have to be shut down. The furnaces then have to be cleared of remnant oil vapour otherwise the boilers may explode on ignition.

    Once the boilers have been started, it takes time to build up high pressure steam. The turbine-generators have to be run-up slowly and it takes about an hour before the generator can transmit any electricity into the system. There are technical and practical limitations to the speed at which the boilers and the turbine-generators can be loaded. This is a slow process because fast loading will damage the turbine-generators. The speed at which supplies are restored has also to be balanced with the rate of generation, otherwise the system might collapse again. That is why restoration of supply could only be started three hours after the system collapsed on 5th February, 1983.

    This procedure of restarting the electricity supply system is universally followed by all utility authorities irrespective of the type of generator or boiler in use.

    Can we prevent a recurrence of the particular fault that led to the island-wide power failure on 5th February, 1983? The answer is obviously yes. We can tighten up the precautions to be taken prior to carrying out testing of new high voltage equipment in the electricity supply system. This is being done by the PUB.

    Can we ensure that we will never ever have an island-wide power failure? The answer, both theoretically and practically, is No - so long as our power stations are interlinked with automatic transfer of load from one station to another as the need arises.

    Why, then, do we not change our system and instead have each power station service a separate, self-contained sub-network? The reason is because such a system has its own grave disadvantages. If a fault develops at one of the power stations, electricity would not be supplied automatically from another power station and the net result is that area blackouts would be more frequent and more prolonged.

    In 1981 and 1982, there were 77 occasions when one or more generators in our three power stations tripped. However, because our power stations are linked in a network, the other generators were able to pick up the load and only on seven occasions were there power failures covering large parts of the Republic. In other words, the interlinking of the three power stations prevented 7O major power failures. This would not have been possible if the three power stations were not interlinked.

    Furthermore, if the stations were not linked the generating capacity of each power station would have to be increased to even out the load if a particular generator in the power station was malfunctioning. This means higher costs and therefore higher electricity tariffs.

    In a linked network system the generating capacity of the power stations are, in effect, pooled thus allowing economical operation since the more efficient generators can be used to supply a large percentage of the total load.

    Taking all things into consideration the PUB, in common with all major electricity supply systems in the world, has decided that the linked network system is superior and, considering that the last island-wide power failure occurred 17 years ago in 1965, I think that the decision is correct.

Secondly, high-rise public and commercial buildings, shopping complexes and hotels are required, under the Building Control Regulations, to instal standby generators, which, during a power failure, will automatically be started up to bring all the lifts down to the first storey and open the doors. The standby generators also operate the fireman's lift, fire-fighting facilities and emergency lighting in the building.
