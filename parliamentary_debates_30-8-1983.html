AIR POLLUTION FROM PIG FARMS IN PUNGGOL
(Measures to reduce)


    4. Dr Lee Chiaw Meng asked the Minister for the Environment and Minister for Communications what action the Ministry of the Environment will take to reduce air pollution caused by the foul air from pig farms in Punggol.

     The Minister for the Environment and Minister for Communications (Mr Ong Pang Boon): Mr Speaker, Sir, pig farming is by nature pollutive and will give rise to malodour. It is not practical to completely eliminate the odour nuisance. However, the nuisance can be minimized through better housekeeping at the farms and improved waste treatment and disposal methods.

    During the day, the odour is less noticeable because the malodour is dispersed and diluted with rising warm air. However, during the evening when the temperature of the air is cooler at the ground level, the malodour does not disperse easily and the smell nuisance becomes more noticeable. Farmers have, therefore, been advised not to wash or flush their pig pens towards the late afternoon to minimize the nuisance.

Mr Rohan bin Kamis asked the Minister for Trade and Industry, with regard to our water supply by the Public Utilities Board, (a) what improvements have been made to-date; (b) what further improvement,g are being planned; (c) what is the status of our supply; and (d) when and what steps will be taken to avoid water shortage in the event of a prolonged dry season.

     Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam:

    (a) Improvements to-date

    PUB has been constantly seeking new water sources and improving its storage and distribution facilities to ensure there is adequate water supply for Singapore. To date, there are 15 water reservoirs, 3 river sources, 8 treatment plants and more than 3,480 kilometres of water transmission and distribution pipelines in the water supply system.

    During 1980-1982 alone, 4 new impounding reservoirs with a combined storage capacity of 31.4 million cubic metres and related works were completed at a total cost of $66.9 million. These are Sarimbun, Murai, Poyan and Tengeh reservoirs. The storage capacities at the existing Seletar and Upper Peirce reservoirs were also increased by 6 million cubic metres at a cost of $2.3 million.

    Over the same period, 303 kilometres of water mains were laid to improve the transmission and distribution network. New service reservoirs with a combined capacity of 306,000 cubic metres were also constructed at Kent Ridge, Nanyang and Bukit Kallang to regulate supply to Lim Chu Kang/Jurong, Ang Mo Kio, Yishun and the North Eastern part of the island.

    (b) Further Improvements

    To cope with the increasing demand for water, PUB plans to spend some $666 million over the next 5 years on water development projects. These projects will enlarge the reservoir storage capacity and extend and improve the transmission and distribution network.

    Amongst the projects in hand are the Sungei Seletar/Bedok Water Scheme, which will be completed in 1984 and the service reservoirs at Bukit Kallang, Jalan Petua and Jalan Mariam, which will be completed by 1986.

LIQUEFIED PETROLEUM GAS


    22. Dr Chau Sik Ting asked the Minister for Trade and Industry, in view of the widespread use of liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) and the possible dangers associated with its use, what steps are being taken to protect the public to curb improper decanting by unscrupulous traders.

     Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam: I am grateful to the Member for Thomson for raising this question. I can assure him that my Ministry is as concerned as he is about the possible dangers that can arise from the use of liquefied petroleum gas (LPG). LPG is a highly inflammable fuel and approximately 60 per cent of households in Singapore use it for their domestic cooking.

    There have been reports of unscrupulous operators decanting LPG from large industrial cylinders into small cylinders using improvised equipment without paying attention to safety. The danger arises when the valves of the cylinders are tampered with during the improper decanting. Gas leakages arising from tampered valves can cause serious accidents endangering not only the operators but also innocent consumers.

    My Ministry takes a serious view of these dangerous practices. We have consulted the oil companies and relevant Government bodies on ways to eradicate improper decanting of LPG and protect the safety of the public. My Ministry has decided that it is in the public's interest that the LPG trade be regulated through licensing of all suppliers and dealers of LPG in Singapore. The licensing will compel suppliers and dealers to run a clean and honest operation and comply with certain safety standards including the use of security-valve sealing. The requirements for suppliers and dealers to be licensed are set out below.

    My Ministry will be enacting a set of Regulations governing the sale of LPG in Singapore under the Restrictions on the Supply, Distribution and Use of Petroleum and Electricity Act, 1973. Anyone who contravenes the Regulations will face a fine of up to $10,000 or imprisonment for a term of two years or both such fine and imprisonment.

    It is proposed to bring the Sale of LPG Regulations into effect on 5 September 1983. To avoid disruption to the trade and inconvenience to consumers, existing suppliers and dealers of LPG will be given a three-month grace period from 5 September 1983 to apply for their licences. The Domestic Trade Section of my Ministry will administer and enforce the Regulations.
