IMPROVED FLOORINGS FOR NEW HDB FLATS


    1. Mr Sia Kah Hui asked the Minister for National Development if the Housing and Development Board will consider completing new flats with tile, marble or parquet floorings so that buyers can move in immediately on receiving the keys.

     The Minister for National Development (Mr Teh Cheang Wan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the HDB will continue its existing practice of providing floor finishes for the kitchens and bathrooms of newly-completed flats, but will not extend standard floor finishes to other rooms. Any standard floor finish provided is unlikely to satisfy the wide range of tastes of flat buyers. Most of them want to select finishes according to their individual preferences and budgets. The HDB's existing practice allows them this flexibility.

    The provision of standard floorings for kitchens and bathrooms is necessary to reduce damage to these wet areas, and hence minimise the possibility of water leakage. Even then, the HDB's experience shows that many owners hack up the standard kitchen flooring and retile them after the minimum 3-year period during which retiling is prohibited. Flat owners have to bear the cost of repairing any damages to the kitchen and toilet floors as a result of hacking. The HDB feels that owners are likely to replace standard floor finishes in other rooms if these are provided. This practice is a waste of both materials and labour. The provision of floor finishes in itself will not enable flat buyers to move in immediately, as there will be other renovation works required, like repainting of walls, installation of window grilles, etc.

RESIGNATION OF BRIG-GEN LEE HSIEN LOONG


    2. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Minister of Defence and Second Minister for Health whether the impending resignation of Brigadier Lee Hsien Loong from the Army was known or expected when he was promoted to that rank.

     The Minister of Defence and Second Minister for Health (Mr Goh Chok Tong): Mr Speaker, Sir, promotions in the Singapore Armed Forces are decided through a strictly defined set of procedures. The present procedures were published in the March 1982 issue of the Pointer, a publication for SAF officers. The factors considered in a promotion are performance on the job, potential for higher level appointments, character, and recommendations of superiors.

    The SAF has a system of regular annual promotion exercises. The process begins around February and ends in June. Promotions are effective from 1st July of the year in question.

    In the 1984 promotion exercise, the SAF Promotions Council recommended a number of promotions including Col Lee Hsien Loong. That recommendation together with others were accepted by the Minister. It was made irrespective of any contemplated resignation because the decision to promote is made purely on the basis of the officer's capacity to contribute to the SAF. Furthermore, unlike the situation in the civilian sector, SAF regulars who leave active service are still members of the SAF. As reservist officers or NCOs, they can expect to play as important a role or, if they upgrade themselves, even a greater role than when they were in active service.

    In addition, the Armed Forces Council convened a Board of Inquiry into the death of PTE V Rajendran. The law provides that the record of proceedings of a Board of Inquiry held pursuant to the Singapore Armed Forces Act, 1972, and Regulations made thereunder, are privileged. Besides security objections in some cases, there could also be legal difficulties, because the witnesses appearing before a Board of Inquiry are not protected by the same rules of evidence which apply in the courts.     However, I recognize that the death of a serviceman, especially a national serviceman, is a matter of grave concern, not only to my Ministry, but also to the general public. In particular, both findings confirmed that the deceased died of bronchopneumonia, and that there had been no ill treatment.

    To ensure that no serviceman is exposed to unnecessary risk during his military service, the SAF has published training and safety directives, prescribing how training should be conducted, and what specific safety precautions must be taken. The Board is chaired by a senior officer from a different unit, or even from a different Ministry, who has nothing to do with the incident. It investigates not only the immediate cause of the death or injury, but also the training the serviceman was involved in, and whether remedial or disciplinary actions are called for.
