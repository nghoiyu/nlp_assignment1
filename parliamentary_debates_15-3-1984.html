ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

PREMATURE SCHOOL LETTERS
(Introduction of compulsory training)


    1. Mr Liew Kok Pun asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education (a) what was the number of premature school leavers by age group and educational level in 1983; (b) how many of them failed to enrol for vocational and technical classes in VITB institutes under its automatic registration scheme; and (c) whether his Ministry will reconsider the need to introduce compulsory training for all premature school leavers to ensure that entrants into the labour market are adequately prepared for further skill upgrading in industry.

     The Minister of State of Education (Dr Tay Eng Soon) (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, the number of premature school leavers at both primary and secondary school levels in 1983 is similar to that in the year before. There were 2,027 premature primary school leavers in 1983. Compared with 8,900 in 1978, the year before streaming was introduced, the dropout rate has gone down by nearly 41/2 times.

    At secondary level, 2,281 pupils dropped out of school last year. Compared with the rate in 1980, the year before streaming was introduced at secondary level, the reduction is nearly four-fold. The overall saving has been 13,600 pupils who would otherwise have dropped out. At this rate, the result of our streaming policy is that nearly one-third of a cohort is saved from dropping out each year.

    Last year, the automatic registration scheme was tried out on a big scale for the first time for the pupils who failed the PSLE and had to leave school. Out of the 2,030 pupils posted to VITB, 75.6% of them have enrolled in VITB courses. The scheme is being extended to pupils who drop out of school at other levels. For comparison, the success rate in attracting premature school leavers to VITB a few years ago was only 20%. VITB has taken follow-up action to contact the 25% who failed to enrol at

    I do not think there is any need at the moment to introduce compulsory training for all premature school leavers.

COST OF EDUCATION
(Particulars)


    2. Mr Liew Kok Pun asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education (a) what was the cost of education over the last five years for each student (i) at the primary, secondary and tertiary levels; (ii) by field of study at tertiary level; and (iii) the percentage increase against 1959 and adjusted for inflation; and (b) how much subsidy in 1983 was given to the education of each student at these levels so that parents can have a better idea of the amount of investment spent on the education of our children.

     Dr Tay Eng Soon (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, the answers to the hon. Member's question would require several tables. I would be happy to provide them to him later. I will instead highlight a few of the points raised by his question.

    Over the past five years, the recurrent cost of education at all levels has doubled. The main increase has been due to salary adjustments.

    In 1983, the cost for educating a child in primary school was $1,255. At secondary and junior college levels, the costs were $2,067 and $2,974 respectively. Since school fees are very low, school education is highly subsidized by the state. .

    At the VITB and Polytechnics, the average costs to train a student are $4,400 and $5,800 per annum respectively. At the university, the average cost is $14,200 per annum. This varies widely, ranging from $8,000 for Accountancy and Business Administration students to $43,300 for the Medical students. Again, as the fees charged by the institutions are fairly low, the cost is highly subsidized.

    In 1959, it cost $125 per annum to provide a place for a child in primary

CAGE FISHING AND SUPPLY OF POULTRY, VEGETABLES AND PORK


    3. Mr Yeo Choo Kok asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education (a) what is the total number of licences issued by the Primary Production Department for cage fishing to date; (b) to what extent this type of fishing has contributed towards meeting the local demand; and (c) whether the Department has any plan to ensure the constant supply of poultry, vegetables and pork at stable and reasonable prices.

     The First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Primary Production Department has so far issued 63 licences for marine cage net fish farms covering a total sea area of 35 hectares. These farms are located off the coast of Pulau Ubin, Serangoon, Punggol and Lim Chu Kang.

    The estimated annual production of fish from these farms is about 1,500 tonnes or 2% of the total estimated annual consumption of 72,500 tonnes. Thus, marine cage net fish culture's contribution towards meeting the demand for fish has been small. The extent to which the contribution will increase depends on the fish farmers themselves. Marine cage net fish culture is capital intensive and, for this reason, has been used primarily to farm high-valued fish and prawns for sale to restaurants and hotels.

    I have outlined in reply to a previous question the Government's current thinking on pig farming. The Government's policy is to encourage liberal trade practices which will facilitate the import of food supplies from the cheapest source. What applies to pig farmers applies equally to producers of poultry and vegetables. They must compete in order to survive.
