DRUG ABUSE AND TRAFFICKING


    1. Encik Mansor Haji Sukaimi asked the Minister for Home Affairs what is the status of the problem of drug abuse and trafficking in Singapore, and what new or improved programmes are being made or planned to battle this menace.

Column: 1476



    The Minister of State for Law and Home Affairs (Prof. S. Jayakumar) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, the drug situation in Singapore, which reached epidemic proportions in 1976, is now contained as a result of six years of relentless effort in pursuing the Supply and Demand Reduction Strategies of Operation Ferret launched on 1st April 1977. We have succeeded in reducing our addict population by half and have broken up the internal illicit drug distribution sys-

Column: 1477

tem in Singapore. The large trafficking syndicate which used to operate in Singapore has been eradicated.

    Heroin, the main drug consumed by our addicts, continued to be scarce in 1982. This was due to our enforcement action. Consequently, its street price remained high. The year saw a slight decrease in the number of drug offenders arrested, compared to 1981. A total of 4,106 offenders, including drug traffickers, was arrested in 1982 as against 4,590 in 1981, a decrease of 484 (or 10.5%). The number of newcomers showed a fairly constant figure, numbering 677 in 1982 as against 787 in 1981. 1982 also saw a slight decrease in the number of drug traffickers arrested - 450 as against 486 in 1981.

    Of the 450 traffickers arrested in 1982, 399 (or 88.7%) were "ant traffickers" who now dominate the trafficking scene. These are traffickers who smuggle in small quantities of drugs across the Causeway into Singapore. They are mainly addicts themselves who push drugs to sustain their own addiction.

    Although the drug situation is contained, it is by no means over. We are in close proximity to one of the biggest drug-producing areas in the world, The Golden Triangle, and there is free movement of people and goods through Singapore, If we relax in our vigilance, a situation similar to pre-April 1977 period can re-emerge.

    A number of measures have been taken to combat the problem. The entry points have been strengthened. In particular, Central Narcotics Bureau has set up a Task Force at the Woodlands Checkpoint to complement Customs' efforts in detecting the illicit smuggling of drugs. Another measure taken is to deny passport facilities to drug traffickers. This will prevent them from resuming their nefarious activities across international boundaries. I should add that this measure has been accepted for implementation by our other ASEAN partners as well.

    Within Singapore, the CNB is using more sophisticated techniques to gather information about traffickers and to identify them. Preventive education is being carried out by SANA through talks, exhibitions and forums. The Ministry of Educa-

COMMERCIAL CRIME DIVISION
(Upgrading and improvement)


    2. Mr Tan Soo Khoon asked the Minister for Home Affairs, in view of the increase in commercial crime, what steps are being taken to upgrade and improve the Commercial Crime Division.

     Prof. S. Jayakumar (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, commercial crime in Singapore is on the rise. Last year alone, some 2,181 commercial crime cases were reported, This is an increase of 23.5% over that for 1981 during which 1,767 cases were recorded.     Officers involved in investigation of commercial crime have been sent for training both locally at the Police Academy as well as overseas to upgrade their skills.     Realizing the importance of upgrading the Commercial Crime Division to cope with the complexities and the rising number of commercial crimes, my Ministry has invited a British Team to make a study. TOURIST ARRIVALS
(Measures to arrest declining rate)


    7. Mr Rohan bin Kamis asked the Minister for Trade and Industry what are the reasons for the declining rate of tourist arrivals in Singapore; what is the impact on our industries especially the hotel industry of this declining rate and what steps are being taken by the Singapore Tourist Promotion Board to attract more tourists to Singapore.

     Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam: Mr Speaker, Sir, in 1982, the number of tourist arrivals to Singapore increased by 4.5%, less than half the growth achieved in 1981. The growth in our tourist arrivals began to slow down significantly in the middle of 1982. In December 1982 and January 1983, we experienced for the first time in many years declines of 3.3% and 2.9% respectively in tourist arrivals.

    There are several reasons for the declining rate of tourist arrivals. The key factor is the recession in the traditional tourist generating markets such as Europe, USA, Australia and some of our Asian markets. The effect of the recession on the tourism industry has not been peculiar to Singapore. Most countries experienced either declines or very low growth in tourist arrivals in 1982. Growth in world tourism slowed down from 1.3% in 1980 to 0.85% in 1982.

    The slower growth is also partly due to the fact that, in terms of cost of hotel

Column: 1487

accommodation, Singapore has become an expensive city to visit compared to other Asian competitor cities. Over the last four years, hotel room rates in Singapore have doubled.

    The hotel industry has felt the impact of the slower growth in tourist arrivals. The average hotel occupancy rate fell from 85.7% in 1981 to 80.8% in 1982. In January 1983, it fell further to 73.2%.

    In line with the fall in tourist arrivals, the growth in real output of the hotel industry dropped from 10.4% in 1981 to 4.5% in 1982. The restaurants and the retail trade sector also showed slower growth in real output of approximately 5% in 1982 compared to a high growth rate of 9% in 1981. Similarly SIA no longer experienced the brisk business of the previous years; the passenger-kilometres covered by SIA grew by only 5%, less than half the rate in 1981.

    To arrest the decline in tourism growth, the following measures have been taken by the Singapore Tourist Promotion Board:

    (i) Firstly, STPB has revamped its marketing and promotion strategy. The Board is placing more emphasis on markets such as USA and Japan which are expected to recover sooner from the recession. STPB has also intensified its promotion in new markets such as South Korea. New marketing tactics which have more direct influence on potential travellers have been introduced. These include postcard campaigns and in-store promotions in large department stores in Europe and Australia. More imaginative publications and promotional materials are also being used, many of which have been of prize-winning quality. More media and TV crews from the major tourist generating countries have been invited to Singapore to generate greater news coverage and awareness on Singapore. To get greater mileage from its marketing promotions, the Board has also stepped up its joint promotion programmes with airlines, hotels, travel agents and other tourist related organizations.
