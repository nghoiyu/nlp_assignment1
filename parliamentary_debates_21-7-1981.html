CHOICE OF SECOND LANGUAGE


    2. Dr Tan Cheng Bock asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education whether parents have any right in the choice of their children's second language.

     The Minister of State of Education (Dr Tay Eng Soon) (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education): Sir, pupils of Malay, Indian and mixed parentage are at present given a choice of Malay, Tamil or Chinese as their second language on enrolment. However, since the beginning of this year, all ethnic Chinese pupils are required to enrol for Chinese as their second language in Primary 1. An opportunity to switch to another second language is given at Primary 4 or at Secondary 1 only after the pupil has been given a fair chance to try CL2 and has shown that he cannot cope with the language.

Column: 107



    Apart from the fact that we want to encourage our pupils to learn something of their cultural origins through the second language, we know from our records that the passing rate for Chinese pupils is much better in CL2 than in other second languages. It is widely believed that ML2 (Malay as a second language) is an easy subject for non-Malays, but this is not the case. For example, in the 1979 'O' level examination, only 53% of the small number of Chinese pupils (705 to be precise) who took ML2 passed with Grade 6 or better, compared with 95% passes for Malay pupils. In 1980, the percentage passes in ML2 were 61% for Chinese pupils as against 99% passes for Malay pupils. These figures consistently indicate that ML2 is by no means an easy subject for Chinese pupils. In contrast, the percentage passes for Chinese pupils in the 'O' level CL2 examination in 1979 and 1980 were 80% and 84% respectively. One reason why Chinese pupils do not do as well in ML2 as Malay pupils is that they do not speak Malay as the home language. Even for those who do, the level of the home language is likely to be much more elementary than the standard of ML2 taught up to 'O' level.

VISUAL DISPLAY UNITS
(Effects on operators)


    4. Mr Lim Boon Heng asked the Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour, with the increasing use of visual display units, if the Ministry of Labour will be undertaking a comprehensive study on industrial health related to the use of such equipment, and if the Ministry intends to issue guidelines for the protection of workers using such equipment.

     The Minister of State for Labour (Dr Wong Kwei Cheong) (for the Minister for Communications and Minister for Labour): Sir, visual display units (VDUs) have been introduced into industry on a wide scale in many countries during the last decade. They have only recently gained popularity in the Republic.

    Experience in other countries shows that, basically, concern about the use of VDUs on health revolves round radiation hazards and eye problems. In the case of radiation hazards, the Radiation Protection Inspectorate of the Department of Scientific Services conducted a test on VDUs in December, 1980, and concluded that they emitted "no hazardous radiation harmful to the general health of an operator". The UK Health and Safety Executive and the relevant authority of other countries like the US also hold a similar view.

    As regards eye problems, the UK Health and Safety Executive has concluded that, while for most people VDUs pose no risk to health, some attention should be paid to certain aspects of their use if eye discomfort is to be avoided. This organization as well as the US National Institute of Occupational Safety and Health have proposed ways to reduce eye complaints.

Column: 109



    There is, therefore, no need to undertake a comprehensive study on health hazards related to the use of VDUs. The Industrial Health Department of the Ministry will continue to monitor the situation regarding the use of VDUs. It will liaise with the Radiation Protection Inspectorate to ensure that radiation levels remain low. The Ministry will issue guidelines to industry to reduce complaints of eye strain in due course.

     Mr Rohan bin Kamis (Telok Blangah): Sir, would the Minister of State elaborate what these guidelines would be?

     Dr Wong Kwei Cheong: Sir, guidelines will be issued in relation to lighting, such as screen and character size, brightness and contrast, work station and general illumination levels, viewing distance and angle, and minimizing screen glare. Guidelines will also be issued in relation to ergonomic aspects, such as keyboard and screen heights, seat heights and leg-room. Guidelines will also be issued in relation to suitable work rest breaks as well as preplacement and annual vision testing.

     The Minister of State of Defence (Dr Yeo Ning Hong) (for the Minister of Defence): Mr Deputy Speaker, Sir, I have been asked by my Minister to reply.

    It has been the practice of the Ministry of Defence (Mindef) to deploy national servicemen, who are not physically fit for strenuous combat duties, in non-combat supportive and administrative services in the Singapore Armed Forces.

    On the suggestion that clauses 26 and 27 provide for very lenient penalties, I suggest that one year's imprisonment is severe enough. If this penalty proves insufficient as a deterrent, then we can always come back to Parliament for heavier penalties.

    In the case of the Member for Kebun Baru, he was concerned about the courses being merely appreciation courses and that the National Computer Board would not conduct examinations which were professional in character. In my statement I have said that the Bill provides for the establishment of a Professional Examinations Syndicate. The provision should be adequate to ensure that the Syndicate would organize or the Board would organize professional examinations for computer systems personnel which would be recognized by the profession in Singapore.

    In the case of the Member for Telok Blangah, I think his general attitude was one of support. But there were some words of caution regarding the control of expenditure which may perhaps be wasted and also about skilled personnel not being mass-produced. These are words of wisdom which I will pass on to the National Computer Board. I think there should be no question that we want the National Computer Board established as soon as possible in order that our brain services can be developed rapidly.
